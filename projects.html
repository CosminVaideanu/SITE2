PhD Thesis

Investigating natural and anthropogenic variability from regional to global scale using the physical mechanisms associated with climate modes.

In the last 150 years, the atmosphere and ocean have warmed, the amounts of snow and ice have diminished and the sea level has risen. In this context and with an increasingly overpopulated planet, climate variability has become a central subject of discussion for the scientific and politic community alike.

Having the potential to group and resolve systematic problems in the climate system, the climate modes represent a modern and useful tool in weather and climatology research alike.

In my research, I use statistical analythical methods on both observed and model-generated data. Specific investigations (in progress): --> Identifying the global signatures (footprints) and the related physical mechanisms of known climate modes of internal viaribility in total, high, medium and low cloud cover fields, at a global and regional scale. --> Separating anthropogenic from solar forcing influence on internal climate modes and identifying the associated physical processes. --> Investigating the physical mechanism of interaction between internal climate modes and the Antcarctic sea ice cover.
